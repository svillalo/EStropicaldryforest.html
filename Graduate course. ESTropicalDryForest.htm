Summary

Connecting mechanistic knowledge studied in Biodiversity Ecosystem Function field with Biodiversity Ecosystem Service (ES) may help to quantify the Ecosystem Function that result in final ES to finally enhance the implementation of conservation policies. However, it is necessary the creation of a bridge to better manage spatial incongruence in the study and interpretation of ecosystem services and ecosystem function. Tripartite network approaches may help to understand the structure of ecosystem services as a product of multiple ecosystem function. This approach may complement the recent idea of the identification of groups of ES commonly positive associated in space, (i.e., ES ‘bundles’  Maes et al. 2012, Gao et al. 2016 ) as a way to link multiple ecosystem function to ecosystem services delivery.  Some multi-mode network properties such as modularity help to discover cohesive groups within the web. Integrating ecosystem function and ecosystem services using this approach may account for how units interact with the subsets of species of different trophic levels and with which ecosystem services. Tropical dry forests are one of the most threatened ecosystems around the world (tropi-dry 2020). The challenge of recovering Colombian tropical dry forest lies fist in understanding the type of ecosystem services that they provide and second, in a plan for the preservation of natural habitats as the most effective action that can be taken to preserve it  (Harrison and Bruna 1999).

 

 

Course Objectives

By the end of this course, the grand students will be able to:

·         Define the concept of ecosystem services, its principles and limitations

·         Understand the key services associated with any resource (e.g., fresh water) through readings and case-studies of tropical dry forests

·         Appreciate the advantages and potential risks of monetizing ecosystem services

·         Integrate tactical advice on mainstreaming this approach into policy and standard government practices

·         Learn how to map ecosystem services with biparite tools in R

Prerequisites

 Basic knowledge on ecosystem functioning and  introductory programming (R)

Course Materials

Students will use open resources (data and software R and Python), including three main textbooks: 1) R for Data Science (Grolemund and Wickham, 2017) 2) An Introduction to Statistical Learning with Applications in R (Witten et al., 2013). 3) Introduction to Data Analysis in R: Hands-on Coding, Data Mining, Visualization and Statistics from Scratch (Zamora et al., 2020).

 

Course structure

The course consists of 5 modules and a personal semestral assignment. The professor will perform PPT presentations at the beginning of the class. Tutorial information will be included to complete the assignments per module. Additional readings will be included to reinforce the topics covered. Students will attend 5 hours in classroom and 5 hours of personal dedication to studying materials, tutorial, additional resources and finishing their final semestral project.

 

Grading:

The course will be graded in 100 points. 60 points will be collected from the module assignments and quiz. The final assignment will be 40 points worth.

 

Program

Module 1: Forest ecosystems: ecosystem theory and processes

Module 2: Anthropogenic impacts: increases in temperature, fire frequency, ozone and carbon dioxide levels; soil acidification and nitrogen deposition.

Module 3: Climate change impacts on tropical dry forest forests

Module 4: Quantitative and Spatially-Explicit Assessment of Ecosystem Services of tropical dry forest

monitor, model and predict their values in space and time. Exploring how existing data (e.g. elevation, climate, land use, remote sensing) can be used to assess Ecosystem Services through mapping and models in R

Module 5: Ecosystem Services into Decision-Making Process

Challenges and some tips for mainstreaming ecosystem services.
